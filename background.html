Introduction to Entropy

Thermodynamics is the branch of physics that describes how energy moves and changes within a system. These principles govern phenomena ranging from a cooling cup of coffee to the long-term evolution of the universe itself.

One of the most important concepts in thermodynamics is entropy. Entropy is often described as the measure of disorder, but more precisely, it is a measure of how many microscopy arrangements (microstates) can produce the same observable condition (macrostate).

A microstate refers to a specific arrangement of every particle within a system. A macrostate refers to the overall condition we observe, such as whether particles are separated or mixed.

An important principle of statistical mechanics is that all microstates are equally likely. Some macrostates can be produced by only a few microstates, while others can be produced by an enormous number of microstates.

All microstates are considered equally likely. However, not all macrostates are equally likely. Some macrostates correspond to a small number of microstates, while others correspond to an extremely large number.

For example, consider a standard deck of playing cards. A brand new deck arrives in highly ordered arrangements, with the cards grouped by suit and rank. There are relatively few arrangements that would be considered as “ordered” in this way. However, there are an enormous number of arrangements that appear random. After repeated shuffling, the deck naturally transitions from an ordered state to one of the vastly more numerous disordered states. The cards are not attempting to become disordered, rather, disordered arrangements are overwhelmingly more probably because there are for more of them.

Because systems naturally evolve toward states that are statistically more probable, they tend to move from ordered configurations toward more disordered configurations over time.

This behavior is formalized in the Second Law of Thermodynamics, which states that the entropy of an isolated system tends to increase over time. Ordered arrangements become less common while more probably arrangements become increasingly likely. As a result, systems naturally progress toward states of greater entropy.

The implications of this principle extend far beyond lab experiments. Stars radiate energy into space. Heat flows from hotter regions to colder regions. Matter and energy become increasingly dispersed. On the largest scales, thermodynamics governs the future evolution of the entire universe.

Current cosmic determinants suggest that the universe is moving toward a state known as heat death. In this state, energy has become so evenly distributed that no significant energy gradients would remain. Without energy differences, processes that produce change, motion, or useful work become increasingly rare. The universe reaches a condition of maximum entropy, where large-scale structure and activity gradually diminish.

In a state of maximum entropy, no usable energy gradients remain to drive large-scale physical processes.
